Okay dedicated *Song of the Week* fans, one of my big ambitions for this project is to have a little bit of variety in the music I showcase here. Don't get it twisted --- it's my blog and it will feature all my biases --- however, I wouldn't want to do something so egregious as to write about two Beatles-adjacent tracks back-to-back. In fact, for most of the last week I'd been planning to talk about some of the music of the incredible Joni Mitchell.

HOWEVER. It's song of *the* week. And this week I found myself with a few hours spare coming off the train at London Paddington. What to do with those hours if not wander up north along Edgware Road[^1] to visit a legendary site --- one that I'd for some reason never taken the time to see before? Of course, I'm talking about the iconic pedestrian crossing where the Beatles shot the cover of *Abbey Road*, one of the most well-known and highly regarded studio albums ever produced.[^2]

Abbey Road can be thought of in two parts. The first part feels more like a normal --- albeit, unusually great --- album, featuring such icons as [Come Together](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=45cYwDMibGo), [Something](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UelDrZ1aFeY), and [Oh! Darling](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9BznFjbcBVs). All of these songs are deserving of their own articles, with the brilliant rhythm section of *Come Together*, Paul's unusually raw vocal work on *Oh! Darling*, and my personal favourite[^3] --- *Something* --- referred to by none other than Frank Sinatra as *"the greatest love song of the past 50 years."*[^4] The second part, from *You Never Give Me Your Money* through to *The End*, is a more experimental medley --- intended to be taken as a whole rather than as individual tracks. Apparently also known as "the long one" during recording sessions, the Abbey Road medley is one of my favourite listening experiences to be found on record. If you read last week's post you'd know I'm a fan of Paul's later *medley-esque* songs, so this is perhaps no surprise.

![Me, looking rather scruffy, at the famous Abbey Road pedestrian crossing; NW8.](/assets/images/abbey-road.jpg)

*I Want You (She's So Heavy)* comes before the medley, but something about it makes it feel very distinct from the "first half" that I talked about earlier. Maybe it's the heaviness of the track, or perhaps just the sheer length --- with its instrumental crescendo-outro building for over three solid minutes. Either way, it sticks out. Both the lyrics and John's delivery are extremely raw. This is actually emphasised by the fact that there aren't many lyrics at all --- in fact, all we get is alternations of *"I want you"* and *"I want you so bad"*, with the occasional *"she's so heavy"* that you can't help but belt out while listening in the car. This song also happens to feature some of the most memorable and unqiue Beatles bass guitar work from Paul McCartney that we ever got the pleasure of hearing. To top it all off, at the height of the buildup, the song ends completely unresolved --- the transition out is so jarring; leaving you so disoriented that you find yourself forced to fall into the easy comfort of [Here Comes the Sun](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GKdl-GCsNJ0), the next song on the album. Great stuff.

A personal memory I associate with this album is picking up a CD copy with some friends at the end of a road trip to [Birmingham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birmingham) in the largest HMV I'd ever been in.[^5] Along with [Rubber Soul](https://www.last.fm/music/The+Beatles/Rubber+Soul), the drive home wouldn't have been the same without it.

Listen to Abbey Road. I'll see you next Friday.

Your time will come, Joni...
